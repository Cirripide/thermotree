About Thermotree

This map shows where a city is hot and where it is green, area by area. Drag the vertical slider to compare the two sides at any spot: heat on the left, vegetation on the right.

Pick a city anywhere in the world, choose a summer year, and the map redraws for that place.

Why this project

Cities trap heat. Asphalt and concrete soak up the sun all day and release it slowly into the night, while trees and grass do the opposite. As the climate warms, urban heat becomes a growing health issue, especially for older residents and people who work outdoors.

The goal of this map is to raise awareness of the need to plant more trees in cities, and to give an initial indication of which areas need them most: the cells that come out both very hot and very low on vegetation. Those are the spots where new trees, parks, or green roofs would have the largest cooling impact, and where the conversation about urban greening should start.

How to read the map

The city is split into a uniform grid of 200 × 200 m cells. Every cell is colored by two things:

  • Left side, Heat. How hot the ground was during the summer's daytime passes, in degrees Celsius. Bright yellow is hot, dark purple is cool. This is surface temperature (rooftops, asphalt, parks, water surfaces themselves), not the air temperature you would read on a weather station.
  • Right side, Vegetation. How green and leafy the cell is, on a 0 to 1 scale (NDVI). Dark green is dense vegetation, grey is bare or built area.

Cells with diagonal stripes have no data for that summer. This typically happens when the satellite simply could not get a clean read of that spot during the three summer months: too much cloud cover, too few clear passes, or no consistent signal across June, July and August.

Use the Summer dropdown to compare years. That is where structural change becomes visible (a new park, a new development) as opposed to the noise of any single summer's weather.

Where the data comes from

  • Heat: NASA and USGS Landsat 8 and 9 satellites, Surface Temperature product (Collection 2, Level 2). 100 m thermal sensor, daytime passes only.
  • Vegetation: ESA Sentinel 2 satellites, surface reflectance product. 10 m red and near infrared bands.
  • City boundary: OpenStreetMap (via Nominatim).

For each summer (June, July and August), the app combines every clear satellite pass over the city into a single median composite. That step automatically filters out cloudy days, smoke, and short lived anomalies. The composite is then averaged inside each 200 m cell.
